Frequently Asked Questions

What is an interest rate?
An interest rate is the percentage of a loan principal charged by the lender as cost of borrowing, or paid by a bank to hold deposits. Expressed as an annual percentage, rates reflect risk, inflation expectations, and central bank policy.
What is the difference between interest rate and APR?
The interest rate is the basic cost of borrowing, excluding fees. APR includes fees, points, and other loan costs spread over the loan term. APR is always greater than or equal to the interest rate and gives a truer picture of total loan cost.
What credit score do I need for a good interest rate?
Generally: 760+ = best rates; 720–759 = very good; 680–719 = good; 620–679 = fair. Even a 0.5% rate difference can mean thousands of dollars over the life of a mortgage or large loan.
How does the Federal Reserve affect my interest rate?
The Fed sets the federal funds rate — the rate banks charge each other overnight. This flows through to prime rate and other benchmarks. When the Fed raises rates, mortgage and credit card rates typically rise; when they cut, rates fall.
